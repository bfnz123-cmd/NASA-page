Lower Earth Orbit: ISS Operations and Spacewalks

While deep-space exploration captures the world’s imagination, humanity's continuous presence in space depends heavily on operations closer to home. Floating 420 kilometers above our heads, the International Space Station (ISS) serves as a critical orbital laboratory and the ultimate testing ground for technologies that will eventually power long-duration voyages to Mars.


Maintaining this massive, football-field-sized infrastructure requires constant vigilance, precision maneuvering, and high-risk extravehicular activities (EVAs)—commonly known as spacewalks.

Recent Spacewalk Chronicles: Keeping the Station Alive

The exterior of the ISS is constantly exposed to cosmic radiation, extreme temperature fluctuations (-120°C to +120°C), and micrometeoroid debris. To combat this harsh environment, astronauts frequently step out of the Quest Airlock into the vacuum of space.

  • Solar Array Upgrades: Recent spacewalks have focused heavily on installing new International Space Station Roll-Out Solar Arrays (iROSAs). These advanced, flexible solar blankets unroll like yoga mats to boost the station's aging power grid, ensuring enough electricity for complex science experiments.

  • The Critical Wrist-Joint Repair: In a highly technical recent orbital surgery, spacewalking astronauts successfully replaced a malfunctioning wrist-joint on Canadarm2—the station's 17-meter-long robotic arm. This critical component is vital for capturing arriving cargo spacecraft and moving heavy equipment outside the station.

Daily Life and Microgravity Science

The ISS is a bustling hive of international science,
holding a crew of seven astronauts who work on a meticulous schedule timed down to the minute.

  • Human Health in Space: Astronauts conduct daily biological research on themselves to study bone density loss, muscular atrophy, and fluid shifts in the human body. Understanding these changes is vital to keeping the Artemis crews healthy during deep-space travel.

  • Materials Science: Microgravity allows scientists to mix metals, grow high-quality protein crystals, and print 3D biological tissues without the distorting effects of Earth's gravity. These experiments pave the way for advanced medicines and stronger materials back on Earth.

The Orbital Stepping Stone

The ISS is not separate from our journey to the Moon; it is an active participant.

  • Testing Life Support: The advanced environmental systems used to recycle 98% of astronaut sweat, urine, and breath into clean drinking water and fresh oxygen on the ISS are directly informing the design of the life-support systems inside the Orion spacecraft and future lunar outposts.

  • An Enduring Partnership: For over 25 years, the ISS has maintained uninterrupted human habitation, proving that global nations can successfully cooperate in the peaceful exploration of space.
